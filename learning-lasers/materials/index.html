Chapter 5

Materials Guide

How a 1064 nm IR fibre laser interacts with different materials — which mark well, which need coating, and which must never be used without ventilation or at all.

Coming soon

Topics this chapter will cover

  • 🔩
    Stainless steel (SS304/316) — the ideal fibre laser material. Thin-film Cr₂O₃ oxidation produces the colour spectrum covered in the SS304 Color Predictor. Brushed, polished, and matte finishes behave differently.
  • ⚙️
    Mild steel and tool steel — marks well with oxide colouring; higher carbon content changes the heat response. Galvanised coatings produce zinc oxide fumes — ventilation required.
  • 🏅
    Aluminium and anodised aluminium — bare aluminium reflects 1064 nm very efficiently and is difficult to mark without a coating (e.g., CerMark, mustard, dry moly). Anodised aluminium marks cleanly by ablating the dye layer.
  • 💍
    Brass and copper — high reflectivity at 1064 nm. Typically requires higher power or coating assistance. Gold and silver similar.
  • 🪵
    Wood and leather — fibre lasers can mark these but are less efficient than CO₂ for cutting. Used for fine engraving; fumes require extraction.
  • 🔬
    Coatings and cermark-type pastes — applied to metals that don't mark directly. The paste absorbs the IR energy and bonds a dark compound to the surface. Wipe off excess after marking.
  • ⚠️
    Never engrave: PVC, vinyl, chlorinated plastics — releases hydrogen chloride gas (HCl), which is toxic and corrosive to the machine optics. Also avoid chrome-plated items without ventilation.
