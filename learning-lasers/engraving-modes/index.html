Chapter 4

Engraving Modes

Vector fill vs raster scan, lines-per-centimetre density, unidirectional vs bidirectional hatching, and 90° crosshatch — how fill strategy determines heat distribution and colour uniformity.

Topics this chapter will cover

  • 📋
    Vector fill mode — the laser fills a closed path with parallel hatch lines. Speed determines dwell time per line; the galvo's flyback speed matters because the beam is on during the return on bidirectional mode.
  • 🖼️
    Raster mode — sweeps the full working area row-by-row, firing only where there is content. Slower for sparse designs, but simpler power mapping for grayscale images.
  • 📏
    Lines per centimetre (LPC) — sets hatch line spacing. 1000 LPC = 0.1 mm between lines (roughly 1 beam-diameter overlap at 0.1 mm spot). Too high = heat build-up; too low = visible scan lines in the result.
  • ↔️
    Unidirectional vs bidirectional — unidirectional fires left-to-right only, allowing the steel to cool on flyback. Bidirectional fires both ways, which can cause visible overheating at the left and right edges of a fill.
  • #
    Crosshatch (90°) — a second fill pass at 90° to the first. Doubles total energy input, multiplies local heat retention, and produces more uniform oxide colour on SS304 — at the cost of longer engrave time.
  • 🌡️
    Heat accumulation at corners — the galvo decelerates and re-accelerates at sharp corners, causing the beam to dwell longer at turn-around points. This produces hot spots visible as colour rings or burn marks.
