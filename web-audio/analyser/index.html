Analyser & Visualiser

AnalyserNode taps into the audio graph without modifying the signal. It exposes raw FFT frequency data and time-domain waveform data via typed arrays — the foundation for any audio visualiser.

How it works

AnalyserNode sits in the signal path (or is connected as a tap) and captures the audio in a circular buffer. It applies a window function (Blackman by default) to reduce spectral leakage before performing the FFT. The node does not modify the signal — it just observes.
FFT size is always a power of two (32–32768). A larger FFT gives better frequency resolution but poorer time resolution — you're averaging over a longer window. At 44100 Hz sample rate, an FFT of 2048 covers 2048/44100 ≈ 46 ms and produces 1024 frequency bins, each 44100/2048 ≈ 21.5 Hz wide.
Smoothing time constant blends successive FFT frames: output = smoothing × previous + (1 − smoothing) × current. At 0.8 the spectrum reacts slowly and looks smooth; at 0 every frame is independent — useful for measuring transient peaks. Typical values: 0.7–0.85 for music visualisers.
Logarithmic frequency axis. Displaying bins linearly would cram musical information into the left 10% of the display — a piano's entire 7 octaves span 27–4000 Hz while 20 kHz sits at the far right. Map bin index to x using x = log(f/20) / log(1000) × W to spread octaves evenly.
